Figure 1 (class_distribution.png): after cleaning, the dataset holds 42,843 phishing and 39,232 legitimate emails, so phishing makes up 52.2% of the data. The near-balance is convenient: accuracy stays a meaningful metric, no resampling is needed, and a stratified split will keep the same ratio in the training and testing sets.

Figure 2 (email_length_distribution.png): legitimate emails average 168 words (median 93, standard deviation 260) while phishing emails average 103 words (median 48, standard deviation 156), so on average the legitimate class runs longer in this corpus. Both distributions pile up on short messages and overlap heavily, which means length alone cannot separate the classes - the signal has to come from the words themselves.

Figure 3 (top_words_per_class.png): the two vocabularies barely overlap - only 7 of the top 20 words are shared (new, please, aug, one, email). The legitimate side is dominated by business and scheduling vocabulary (ect, would, submissionid, list, subject, message), while the phishing side is dominated by promotional and action vocabulary (top, company, money, us, news, account). Compared with the fake news project, where both classes shared the same political core, these classes speak almost different languages - an early hint that the classification task should be easier here.

Figures 4-5 (wordcloud_legit.png, wordcloud_phishing.png): the word clouds present the same frequency counts as Figure 3 in a form that works well on a slide. The legitimate cloud is built from workplace nouns and scheduling words; the phishing cloud is built from money, offers and calls to action. They add presentation value rather than new information.

Figure 6 (phishing_keywords.png): every one of the 15 classic scam words appears more often in phishing than in legitimate mail. The most frequent in phishing is 'click' (14.7% of phishing emails vs 4.8% of legitimate), and the biggest class gap belongs to 'money' (14.4% vs 2.6%). The keyword list was fixed before looking at the data, so this is a fair check rather than cherry-picking. At the same time, no single word is anywhere near universal, which is exactly why the models will use thousands of word features instead of a keyword rule.
